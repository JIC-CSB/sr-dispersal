Stem rust of wheat

Stem rust is known as the “polio of agriculture” due to the severe damage it can inflict on wheat crops leading to complete crop loss if left untreated. The disease is caused by a fungus called Puccinia graminis f. sp. tritici that spreads between wheat plants by creating thousands of spores (called urediniospores) that blow on the wind to start new infections. Each spore that is created is a clone and thereby closely resembles the original fungal strain, with all its features.

What is the role of Berberis?

Once wheat starts to mature at the end of the growing season in colder climates like the UK the fungus enters a dormant state on plant debris in the field to enable it to survive over the winter. When it awakes in the spring it then infects a different plant called Berberis. This shrub can occur in hedgerows alongside wheat fields and is where the fungus undergoes sexual reproduction. When it does this, it shuffles genetic material between two strains and makes new genetic combinations.

These new fungal strains that are created on Berberis plants are different to the original ones and may have new characteristics such as being able to infect previously resistant wheat varieties. Once this stage is complete, the fungus then creates a new type of spore (called aeciospores) that spreads these new strains into neighbouring wheat fields to once again start infection in wheat crops.

How can mathematical modelling help?

The crucial role of Berberis plants in creating new strains of the fungus historically led to their removal in Europe and northern USA, particularly the removal of B. vulgaris (common barberry) that is very susceptible to stem rust infection. Today, with limited reports of stem rust infecting wheat in the UK we can be more strategic and use spore dispersal modelling to work out which Berberis bushes could harbour the stem rust pathogen and need careful monitoring.

This is where mathematical modelling can help. We can use maths to determine the likely distance spores can travel on the wind from an infected Berberis bush, and thereby the risk those bushes pose as sources of infection for wheat crops in the area. As the spores that are created on Berberis bushes blow on the wind to infect wheat plants, environmental factors such as wind speed are also important for the model.

As stem rust of wheat is re-emerging as a disease of concern in western Europe it is vital that we develop a map of where high risk bushes occur so they can be checked for infection and monitored as this fungus increases in concern in the UK.
